THIS IS THE ORIGINAL DECK, ANYTHING ELSE NOT DIRECTLY MENTIONED HERE OR ON THE GITHUB WAS DONE WITHOUT MY KNOWLEDGE, INCLUDING ANY AI OR PAID MODIFICATIONS TO THE DECK.

Kaishi 1.5k is a modern Japanese deck in the spirit of Core 2k but with additional features, better coverage optimized through frequency sorting and a modular nature. You can read about the origin of the deck here. You can see the various deck options here. Please use the GitHub page to report issues with the deck. If you have an issue with the deck, chances are others will as well.

NOW WITH PICTURES!

If you struggle with kanji, consider doing this related radicals deck.

Disclaimer: This deck is meant to jump-start your immersion. Once you are done with the deck, you are encouraged to start immersing in the language using Yomitan and similar tools. For a guide explaining how immersion works, please see my guide to learning Japanese.

on 2025-10-13
The choice of vocabulary in this deck is great. Having gone through almost half of the deck (plus some grammar and input), I'm starting to get the gist of most (mundane) Japanese sentences and expressions. The cards mostly have all the information that is needed but not too much. You will also conveniently learn to read a decent number of kanji on the side, especially if you set up the Kanji Popup Dictionary in Anki. It's not a perfect deck though: Some (and only some) words with multiple/wide meanings have been split into multiple cards without obvious logic which gets confusing during reviews. The images are all over the place and range from ok to actively misleading. I heavily recommend hiding the images from the card layout. The example sentences also range from ok to awful: Some sentences have nothing but unfamiliar words (that often aren't even introduced in the deck), some are so off-kilter that they don't really do much for comprehension. Still, it's better to have the sentences than not, as most will provide at least a bit of context for the use of the word. Despite the criticisms, this decks is a good learning tool. I do recommend hiding the sentence on the card front by making it a Hint since otherwise you'll just end up remembering the words in that context rather than learning the word itself. It's also not a bad idea to make the sentence translation on the card back a Hint so you can try to figure out the sentence on your own first.

I've used a lot of different Japanese vocab decks before. Some of them are really popular, others not so much. But out of everything I’ve tried so far, the KAISHI 1.5k deck is honestly the one I like the most. The reason is simple: this deck forces you to actually learn the words properly if you want to make progress. The cards are kept super clean and minimal, which helps a lot because there’s no distracting extra info all over the place. You just focus on the vocab and the example sentences. What I highly recommend is reading the example sentences out loud, trying to translate them yourself first, and only then flipping the card. If you do that, you’ll not only remember the words faster, but you’ll also get a solid feeling for how they’re used naturally in real sentences. In my opinion, this is one of the best decks out there for building real, usable vocab. Sure, sometimes there are words that you probably won’t hear often or at all - but that shouldn’t discourage anyone. The deck still contains a ton of super common, everyday words. Honestly, even after the first 10 to 200 words, you’ll already start noticing them constantly when watching japanese stuff. Sometimes every two to three minutes you’ll hear something you already know, and that’s super motivating.

Composing this review has been a challenging task, primarily because I wish to acknowledge the considerable effort invested in crafting the Kaishi 1.5k deck. It's a testament to the caring nature of the TMW community toward beginners. However, I find myself compelled to address certain shortcomings. Despite its merits, I believe there are better options available, especially for beginners. Approaching this review from the perspective of an intermediate learner who has traversed various beginner decks and lived with their flaws, I must emphasize the deck's fundamental philosophy as its main drawback. For novice learners whose grasp of grammar and language skills is still evolving, mastering vocabulary should ideally occur through sentences introducing easily digestible concepts rather than isolated, frequently encountered words - an approach more suited to intermediate and advanced learners. This pedagogical approach, exemplified by decks like Tango as opposed to Core2.3k/Kaishi, focuses on achieving what I would term as "sentence level i+1". While Tango may lack in teaching relevant/common words compared to Core2.3k/Kaishi, it excels in presenting its vocabulary in a context that facilitates comprehension. For instance, a deck that in my experience effectively balances word ordering, common vocabulary, and sentence level i+1 comprehension is JLAB. Despite being categorized as a "grammar deck," JLAB adeptly covers all essential aspects for beginners, facilitating the understanding of curated anime/jdrama sentences. However, I caution against utilizing its listening component, recommending instead a direct dive into its reading material. JLAB has its drawbacks still, such as limited vocabulary coverage and a suboptimal card format, but from a content perspective, I have little gripe with it. [Link to JLAB: https://ankiweb.net/shared/info/911122782] In summary, while Kaishi 1.5k possesses commendable attributes, it ultimately falls short by discarding the strengths of decks like Tango from which it was derived. It fails to deliver on the crucial aspect of sentence level i+1 comprehension, thereby resembling a glorified rendition of Core2.3k or jpdb, when it can be so much more. Still good deck though.
